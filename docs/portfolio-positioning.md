# Portfolio Positioning

Race Signals should be built as a serious job-application clip.

It should connect Trevor Brown's investigative reporting background with modern newsroom product and data engineering work.

## Read Of The Existing Portfolio

Trevor's public portfolio positions him as an investigative journalist, developer and data visualizer.

The strongest pattern across the current work is public records turned into usable systems:

- Open Cabinet tracks executive-branch financial disclosures and stock trades.
- Capitol Releases archives Senate press releases with provenance and deletion tracking.
- FOIA Tracker turns FOIA.gov data into agency accountability rankings.
- News Pulse monitors trusted open-web sources for early news movement.
- Second Brain shows large-scale personal data ingestion, search and local AI tooling.

Race Signals should extend that pattern into campaign finance.

## What Race Signals Should Prove

This project should prove four things quickly:

- Trevor can identify a newsroom problem before it becomes a dashboard request.
- Trevor can turn messy public data into a stable, source-linked product.
- Trevor can write clear methodology and caveats.
- Trevor can design for reporters and readers, not just for engineers.

The clip should not try to look like a national election data platform. It should look like a focused intelligence tool that a newsroom data team could realistically use.

## Fit For TEGNA-Style Roles

The public TEGNA data and graphics roles emphasize data-driven reporting, useful visual storytelling, collaboration with reporters and product teams, and clear audience-facing explanations.

Race Signals should map directly to that:

- Data Journalist: surfaces story leads from official records.
- Data Producer and Researcher: normalizes source data and strengthens reporting with evidence.
- Graphics and Data Visualization Journalist: turns complicated campaign finance changes into readable cards, profiles and explainers.
- Product partner: creates reusable views that can support many local markets.

The strongest TEGNA angle is local-market utility.

Future expansion should make it easy to filter by state or media market, so a local newsroom can ask: what changed in our races this week?

## Positioning Line

Short version:

> Race Signals is a campaign finance intelligence feed that helps reporters spot early money movement in federal races before it becomes an obvious story.

Application version:

> I built Race Signals to show how I would approach newsroom data product work: start with an official public-records source, narrow the scope, build a reliable ingestion pipeline, generate transparent story leads, and present them in a way reporters can actually use.

## How It Should Differ From Existing Projects

Compared with Open Cabinet:

- Race Signals should be faster-moving and feed-first.
- It should focus on "what changed" rather than profile pages alone.

Compared with Capitol Releases:

- Race Signals should reuse the same provenance-first discipline.
- It should show financial-data normalization rather than web archive collection.

Compared with FOIA Tracker:

- Race Signals should be more event-driven.
- It should turn individual records into story leads, not only rankings.

Compared with News Pulse:

- Race Signals should share the same monitoring instinct.
- It should rely on official records rather than social or open-web source monitoring.

Compared with Second Brain:

- Race Signals should show similar ingestion and search discipline.
- It should be public, focused and job-reviewer friendly.

## Clip Acceptance Test

Before linking this in applications, the site should answer these questions without explanation from Trevor:

- What is this?
- What data does it use?
- What changed recently?
- Why does this item matter?
- Can I verify it from the source?
- Can I find a candidate or committee?
- Can I understand the limits?
- Does this feel like something a newsroom could use?

If the answer to any of those is no, keep tightening the product.

## Do Not Overplay

Avoid these claims:

- Real-time campaign finance monitoring
- Complete national coverage
- Definitive competitiveness scoring
- Proof of wrongdoing
- AI-powered truth finding

Better:

- Latest available FEC records
- Narrow 2026 Indiana House MVP
- Deterministic signal rules
- Reporter leads
- Source-linked public records

## Public-Safe Project Framing

For the site itself:

> Race Signals monitors Federal Election Commission records for notable campaign finance activity in a narrow race universe. It translates new filings, committee activity, receipts, disbursements and outside spending into source-linked story leads for reporters.

For the README:

> This is a newsroom data engineering project, not a prediction model. The goal is to make campaign finance changes easier to spot, verify and explain.

For interviews:

> The product is deliberately narrow. I wanted to show that I can ship a clean, auditable civic-data tool instead of promising a giant election platform.
